1. The Injection Moulding Bottleneck

An imaginary phone manufacturer (phonesRus) has approached us to produce the top and bottom casings for their mobile devices. Typically the main manufacturing method for the production of mobile phone casings is injection moulding and with good reason. Injection moulding has significant benefits such as high repeatability and high speed manufacturing however the potential disadvantages can be devastating.

Adhesives Technologies is a company that develops bonding chemicals that act as alternatives to bolts and welds and their case offers some insight into what can happen when injection moulding goes wrong. To achieve the strong bonding properties of their chemicals, highly reactive additives are required and in this case, the additives became overly corrosive when heated up to the moulding temperature. The additives began ‘consuming’ the tooling effectively altering the precision of the mould resulting in incompatible components. Furthermore, the multiple heat cycles further propagated the damage causing the tooling faces of each half to shift. To make matters worse, the pressure release valves were insufficient because the additives produced more vapour than could be released, literally burning the mould. The impact of this damage meant that the tools needed to be rebuilt which resulted in a delay where what used to be a 12 week lead time became a 24 week delay.

Incorporating industry 4.0 technologies such as additive manufacturing and digital twins can help to stretch the possibilities rendered impossible by the rigidity of injection moulding. Industry 4.0 can be defined as the intelligent networking of processes and machinery in industry using data [1]. A major attribute of industry 4.0 is that it provides a shift from centralised manufacturing to de-centralised manufacturing, contributing potential solutions to setbacks such as the one mentioned above. Merit3D used photopolymer carbon DLS (digital light synthesis) to rapidly prototype iterations until they got the final result, a process that took a single day. Within three days the hangers were on the production line using high speed printing technology to print thousands of parts per day. Merit3D proved that additive manufacturing can replace traditional manufacturing methods such as injection moulding and as such, it will be the method of manufacture that we will utilise in this undertaking.

Inventory Sawtooth Monitoring

One of the most important parts of this digital twin was figuring out if the 3D printer fleet could actually keep up with the Festo line. I set up a "Sawtooth" monitoring chart to track our buffer levels. The reason it looks like a sawtooth is because of how differently these two worlds work.

The printers work in big "Push" batches. Every 14 hours, a printer finishes 6 parts and they all get dumped into the buffer at once—that's the sharp vertical jump you see on the chart. But the Festo line is doing "Pull" manufacturing; it takes one part every 6 minutes like clockwork. That constant drain causes the slow, steady downward slope. My goal was to make sure that even at the very bottom of the "tooth," we never hit zero. If we hit zero, the Festo line "starves" and the whole factory stops, which would be a disaster for our 100,000 unit target.

Resource & Bottleneck Analysis

After running the simulation, I looked into the resource statistics to see how hard the equipment and staff were actually working. It was interesting to see that while the Oven Station and the Operator Station were the busiest parts of the loop, there's still a massive amount of "Waiting" time across the whole system.

Worker Statistics

The data shows the worker is "Waiting" for about 80% of the time. This confirms that the line is highly automated, but it might also mean I could give this worker more tasks, like managing the 3D printer fleet maintenance, without slowing down the operations.

Station Utilization

The Oven Station is the most utilized at around 15%, followed by the Operator Station. This proves that our buffer capacity of 158 units is more than enough to keep the line fed without any starving errors.

5. Value Stream Analysis

To reconcile the 14-hour batch processing of additive manufacturing with the 6-minute tactical demand of the FESTO automation line, I had to design a "Leaned-out" value stream. The core of this strategy was the FIFO queue architecture.

The 14-hour recovery lead time is the main bottleneck. By using a fleet of 23 printers for the bottom cases, we create a staggered arrival of parts. Even if one printer fails, the buffer of 158 units acts as a safety float, preventing the Festo line from shutting down. This allows us to transition from "Push" manufacturing at the printers to "Pull" manufacturing at the automation line.

1. Strategic Context: Week 1

An imaginary phone manufacturer (phonesRus) has approached us to produce the top and bottom casings for their mobile devices. Typically the main manufacturing method for the production of mobile phone casings is injection moulding and with good reason. Injection moulding has significant benefits such as high repeatability and high speed manufacturing however the potential disadvantages can be devastating. Adhesives Technologies is a company that develops bonding chemicals that act as alternatives to bolts and welds and their case offers some insight into what can happen when injection moulding goes wrong. To achieve the strong bonding properties of their chemicals, highly reactive additives are required and in this case, the additives became overly corrosive when heated up to the moulding temperature. The additives began ‘consuming’ the tooling effectively altering the precision of the mould resulting in incompatible components. Furthermore, the multiple heat cycles further propagated the damage causing the tooling faces of each half to shift. To make matters worse, the pressure release valves were insufficient because the additives produced more vapour than could be released, literally burning the mould. The impact of this damage meant that the tools needed to be rebuilt which resulted in a delay where what used to be a 12 week lead time became a 24 week delay. Moreover the financial losses were also substantial, from the capital loss from the damaged moulds to the emergency remachining of the mould which possibly incurred a premium cost because of material upgrade and expedition fees, this loss was definitely felt. Further losses may have included wasted polymers from failed cycles and contractual penalties. This case study maintains that though injection moulding is very reliable, it can be very inflexible when things go wrong.

Incorporating industry 4.0 technologies such as additive manufacturing and digital twins can help to stretch the possibilities rendered impossible by the rigidity of injection moulding. Industry 4.0 can be defined as the intelligent networking of processes and machinery in industry using data [1]. A major attribute of industry 4.0 is that it provides a shift from centralised manufacturing to de-centralised manufacturing, contributing potential solutions to setbacks such as the one mentioned above. By incorporating additive manufacturing as the industry 4.0 solution we can address these issues with great success as outlined by Merit3D’s remedy for the Additive technology kerfuffle. Because of the tooling issues, Adhesives technology was missing the plastic hangers required to make them ready for retail. They reached out to Merit3D, an additive manufacturing service provider based in Utah, to rapidly manufacture these hangers and completely bypass the need for a mould. Merit3D used photopolymer carbon DLS (digital light synthesis) to rapidly prototype iterations until they got the final result, a process that took a single day. Within three days the hangers were on the production line using high speed printing technology to print thousands of parts per day. Merit3D proved that additive manufacturing can replace traditional manufacturing methods such as injection moulding and as such, it will be the method of manufacture that we will utilise in this undertaking.

The Four Pillars of Industry 4.0

  • Cyber to physical systems - where physical objects are digitally represented. I will be using CAD software (fusion) to design the casings.
  • Internet of things - a network of sensors that allow devices and systems to run autonomously without human intervention. I will be using the FESTO lab to simulate a production pass of my casing. The FESTO lab is a fully autonomous conveyor system that will conduct all the measurements and processes with minimal human intervention.
  • Cloud computing - The storage and analysis of large data sets on large remote servers that provide the computational power required for strenuous processing.
  • Artificial intelligence - The use of AI for efficient pattern recognition and process optimisation. I will be using Siemens Technomatix plant simulation to create a digital twin.

Slicing Engine Optimization: Arachne

The top case was a much more printer friendly design than the bottom case and did not encounter much optimisation. That being said, there was an issue during the slicing of the snap fit. The high precision snap-fit clips that were designed in fusion appeared fragmented in the slicer preview. This was a consequence of the ‘classic wall generator’ having a fixed extrusion width of 0.42mm. Because the snap-fit was thinner than the nozzle diameter in certain regions, the slicer software deemed it unprintable, forming fragments. To fix this issue, the wall generator was changed from ‘classic’ to ‘Arachne). Arachne works differently to the classic wall generator by varying the line width according to the geometry of the model. This allowed it to close the gaps forming solid snapfits.

Bridging Optimization Trial

I came to the conclusion that the best sure fire way to reliably manufacture these phone cases with minimal post processing would be to print them lying flat on their back without supports as demonstrated by V2. The issue with V2 was that the bridging was inadequate and as a result I undertook some optimisation tests to identify the ideal bridging settings. I wanted to keep the overhang and bridge speeds constant and have the layer height as my independent variable but first I had to make some adjustments to the overhang speeds to ensure ideal conditions. I decreased the overhang speed [50%,75%] from 30mm/s to 20mm/s and the bridge speed to 25mm/s to ensure that the second half of the overhang (which is practically printing over thin air) prints slow enough for the PLA to achieve its glass temperature almost instantly.

4. Digital Twin Simulation

Once I confirmed the fit of both the top and bottom parts, it was time to explore the feasibility of expanding this process by an industrial scale; 100,000 parts (50,000 full sets) a year. Using the Siemens Tecnomatix Plant Simulation software, I modeled how the Festo lab would interact with the 3d printers to investigate the process. First I needed to determine the number of printers that would be required for this process. An assumption of a 250 day industrial calendar was made to account for UK bank holidays, downtime, annual leave and weekends. Otherwise the plant would function on a 24/7 basis. A 10% failure factor was implemented to account for any failed prints as well as an effective availability factor of 95% which was used to estimate the actual uptime of the printers.

// 1. Global Fleet Capacity Formula

Required Fleet = (Annual Volume × Print Time (mins) × Scrap Factor (1.11)) / Operational Minutes per Machine

// 2. Bottom Cases (50,000 units)

Total Load: 50,000 × 139 mins × 1.11 = 7,714,500 total load minutes

Required Units: 7,714,500 / 342,000 = 22.55 (23 Units Required)

// 3. Top Cases (50,000 units)

Total Load: 50,000 × 73.8 mins × 1.11 = 4,095,900 total load minutes

Required Units: 4,095,900 / 342,000 = 11.97 (12 Units Required)

The festo simulation was designed such that 23 printers would be used to print the bottom cases and 12 printers would be used to print the top cases. The bottom cases required measurement at the measuring station whereas the top cases did not. From this I gathered that I could create two sources, one that fed into the mag station (bottom cases), and one that fed into the operator station (top cases). Each source assumed a rate of production of 6.04 min and 6.15 min per bottom and top part respectively.

This may sound counterintuitive because the printers technically take roughly 14hrs to print a batch of 6 bottom cases however, by employing a staggered approach and adding a buffer after each source, a steady stream of parts could be produced roughly every 6 minutes. A buffer should contain at least a whole fleet's worth of parts to account for a possible failure in the staggered system.

Bottom Case Buffer Logic
  • Fleet Batch: 23 printers × 6 parts = 138 parts
  • Recovery Lead Time: 14 hours (Total reprint time)
  • Consumption Rate: 10 parts/hour
  • Safety Float (2h): 20 units
  • Total Buffer Capacity: 158 units
Top Case Buffer Logic
  • Fleet Batch: 12 printers × 6 parts = 72 parts
  • Recovery Lead Time: 73.8 minutes
  • Consumption Rate: 10 parts/hour
  • Safety Float (2h): 20 units
  • Total Buffer Capacity: 92 units

The buffer capacity will be maintained by IoT sensors that will be triggered if the capacity reaches an 80% reduction by which a signal will be sent to a standby printer that will print until the levels reach 50% reduction. This ensuring that the line never "starves" while also avoiding excessive overproduction.

7. Pricing & Economic Feasibility

Looking at the money side of things, the Elegoo Centauri Carbon 2 was a complete game-changer. For a Master's project like this, it showed that you don't need a massive budget to hit Industry 4.0 targets. Traditional injection moulding would have cost around £100,000 just for the initial tools, and every time I needed to fix a snap-fit issue, I'd be looking at weeks of delay and thousands in extra costs.

Instead, the printer only costs about £350. Even my whole fleet of 35 printers would cost less than a quarter of a single mould. PLA filament is pennies per part—roughly £18-20 per kilo. It meant I could iterate seven different versions for less than the price of a coffee. This project proves that agile manufacturing using accessible hardware is the future of small-batch industrial design.

8. Reflection

Looking back at the whole loop, from the first "support welding" disaster to the final Festo pass, it's clear that the Digital Twin isn't just a fancy animation—it's a tool for predicting real-world failure. Seeing the 80% worker idle time in the stats was interesting; it made me realize the worker isn't just a laborer anymore; they are a supervisor. They handle the fleet, clear the jams, and keep the data accurate. It's a huge shift in how we think about factory work, and it's what makes this Industry 4.0 approach so resilient.
